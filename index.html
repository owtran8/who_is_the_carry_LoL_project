Who Really Carries?

Measuring role-adjusted impact in 2025 professional League of Legends esports

Step 1

Introduction

This project studies professional League of Legends esports data from Oracle's Elixir. My main goal is to understand which roles most often produce the strongest player performance on winning teams.

The main question is: Among winning teams, which role is most likely to be the main impact player?

I use the term main impact player instead of only “carry” because players can help win in different ways. A bot laner may carry through damage, while a support may carry through assists, vision, and low deaths.

What Does Role-Adjusted Impact Mean?

A normal carry score based only on kills, damage, or gold would usually favor bot and mid laners. That would be unfair because each role has a different job.

To fix this, I compare each player only to other players in the same role across the dataset. For example, supports are compared to other supports, junglers are compared to other junglers, and bot laners are compared to other bot laners.

This does not mean I only compare the two players with the same role in one game. It means I compare each player to the overall distribution of players in that same role.

role-adjusted impact = average of role-based performance percentiles
Step 2

Data Cleaning and Exploratory Data Analysis

The dataset has both player rows and team summary rows. Since this project is about individual player impact, I mainly use the player rows where the position is top, jungle, mid, bot, or support.

I also create new columns for player impact, such as kill participation, KDA, damage per minute, vision per minute, and role-adjusted percentiles.

Step 4

Hypothesis Testing

I tested whether all five roles are equally likely to be the main impact player among winning teams.

Null hypothesis: Top, jungle, mid, bot, and support are equally likely to be the main impact player. Each role should appear about 20% of the time.

Alternative hypothesis: Some roles are more likely than others to be the main impact player.

I used Total Variation Distance (TVD) to compare the observed role distribution to the expected equal 20% distribution.

Step 5

Framing a Prediction Problem

For the prediction part, I am building a binary classification model. The model predicts whether a player is their team’s main impact player.

  • 1 = main impact player
  • 0 = not main impact player

Since the label is based on completed-game performance, the model is not trying to predict the carry before the match starts. It is classifying completed player performances.

Step 6

Baseline Model

My baseline model will use Logistic Regression with simple post-game features such as position, kills, deaths, assists, team kills, and team deaths.

The position column is categorical, so it will be one-hot encoded in an sklearn Pipeline.

Baseline model results will be added after the model is trained.

To improve the model, I plan to add engineered features such as KDA, kill participation, damage per minute, vision per minute, and role-adjusted performance percentiles.
